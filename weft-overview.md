# Weft — an introduction in plain language

## What is Weft?

Weft is a way to find your people without a social media platform standing in the middle.

You hold a button and say what you're looking for — "I want to find people experimenting with koji," "I'd love a small group that runs slow on Sunday mornings," "I'm trying to learn banjo and I need patient humans." Your ask travels quietly from friend to friend to friend, the way a question travels when you ask someone at a dinner table and they say "oh, you should talk to my cousin." When it finds someone who fits, you're each shown what the other *is* — a small group, a fellow beginner — but not who they are. If you both say yes, names unlock at the same moment, along with the chain of real people who vouch for each of you. Then you just… talk.

The name comes from weaving: the warp is the fixed threads, and the weft is the thread that travels across them, binding everything into cloth. Your relationships are the warp. Your asks are the weft.

## Why does this need to exist?

Because somewhere along the way, "connecting people" became a business of *standing between* people.

Today's platforms sit in the middle of every relationship they host. They decide what you see, in what order, and how often — not to serve you, but to keep you scrolling, because your attention is what they sell. They know everything about you because knowing everything about you *is the product*. Finding people who share your interests means broadcasting yourself into a feed and hoping an algorithm — tuned for engagement, not for you — makes the introduction. And you can't leave without losing everyone, which is exactly how they like it.

Weft starts from a different question: what if social connection worked like email instead of like television? Email isn't a place you go — it's a channel anyone can use, no company in the middle of your correspondence. Weft is that idea for finding and meeting people: a channel, not a destination. Nobody's business model sits between you and your people, because there is no middle for a business model to occupy.

## What it does

**It finds people through people.** Your ask hops through your actual social graph — friends, then friends of friends, up to a handful of steps out. That's not a limitation; it's the feature. Every match comes with a human trust line you can read: *"the path to them runs through Maya."* Research going back decades says most humans are only about six introductions apart. Weft just makes the introductions ask-able.

**It protects you until you choose otherwise.** Your ask travels without your name on it — even your closest friend can't tell whether an ask passing through them is yours or someone's three hops away. Sensitive questions can travel more privately still, or go to spaces where everyone is verified-real but nobody is identified. Nobody learns who you are until you both agree, at the same instant. Declining is invisible — the other person simply never hears anything, because the system literally contains no message for "no."

**It carries any medium — after you've both said yes.** Once you're connected, share photos, voice notes, video, and long writing freely, end-to-end encrypted, with location metadata stripped by default. You can even ask *with* media — photograph a mystery plant and ask "who else grows this?" — and only the photo's mathematical meaning travels the network; the image itself never leaves your phone until you choose to share it with someone you've matched. What media never becomes is a broadcast: there are no stories, no reels, no gallery for strangers.

**It lets communities run themselves.** Groups have short, human-readable house rules you agree to at the door — written by the group, not by a trust-and-safety department on another continent. A group that governs itself badly loses members to a better one, which is more accountability than any platform has ever faced.

**It stays out of your pocket and out of your data.** No ads, ever — the servers involved are dumb encrypted mailboxes that can't read anything, so there is nothing to sell. Anyone can run one for about the cost of a coffee a month. The app is designed to be *comfortable closed*: its only measure of success is whether you found your people, never how long you stared at it.

## What it doesn't do

- **No feed.** There is nothing to scroll. Ever. When nothing's happening, the app is quiet.
- **No broadcasting.** Weft is not for building an audience, going viral, or becoming an influencer. There are no followers, likes, or view counts.
- **Not instant.** Your ask travels as your friends' phones come online — answers usually arrive within a day or two. Finding the right person is not a same-second problem, and Weft doesn't pretend it is.
- **No algorithmic discovery.** Weft will never say "people also liked…" It doesn't know what you like. It knows what you *asked*, because you said it, on purpose.
- **No rescue from an empty room.** Weft works in proportion to your community using it — it's designed to be adopted by real communities (a club, a congregation, a mutual-aid network) rather than by isolated individuals one at a time.
- **No central police.** There's no corporation to appeal to and no global ban button. Communities set and enforce their own rules; serious crimes remain matters for actual law enforcement, with tools to preserve evidence. This is a genuine tradeoff, chosen openly: the same design that means no one can read your messages means no one can scan everyone's messages.

## How it compares

| | Facebook / Instagram | X / TikTok | Reddit / Discord | Nextdoor | Mastodon / Bluesky | Signal / WhatsApp | **Weft** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **What it's for** | Keeping a profile, following people | Broadcasting to audiences | Topic communities | Neighborhood | Broadcasting, federated | Talking to people you already know | **Finding people you don't know yet, through people you do** |
| **Who decides what you see** | Engagement algorithm | Engagement algorithm | Mods + votes + algorithm | Feed + algorithm | Your follows (mostly) | Nobody — it's just messages | **Nobody — there's nothing to see but your own asks and answers** |
| **How you find new people** | Suggestions, ads, search | Virality | Join big rooms, hope | Proximity | Follow graphs | You don't | **Ask out loud; the answer travels your real social graph** |
| **Who holds your data** | The company | The company | The company | The company | Your chosen server's admin | Mostly your device | **Your device; the network sees sealed envelopes that expire** |
| **Business model** | Ads (you're the product) | Ads | Ads / subscriptions | Ads | Donations / goodwill | Owned by nonprofit / Meta | **None in the middle: communities run their own $5 mailboxes** |
| **Trust & identity** | Blue checks, follower counts | Follower counts | Karma scores | Address verification | Instance reputation | Phone numbers | **Real people vouching for real people, readable as sentences — never scores** |
| **What "success" means to it** | Your time on feed | Your time on feed | Your time in threads | Engagement | Varies by server | Messages delivered | **You found your people, and closed the app** |

The closest cousins deserve a fair word. **Mastodon and Bluesky** decentralize *publishing* — they're better Twitters, and genuinely so — but they keep the feed, the follower counts, and the broadcast model; Weft decentralizes *introduction* instead. **Signal** perfected private conversation between people who already know each other; Weft is for the step before that — becoming people who know each other — and happily hands the conversation off once you've met.

## The honest fine print

Weft is young, and it's slower than a feed on purpose. It needs your community more than it needs you alone — the first thing to do with it isn't to sign up, it's to bring it to a group you're already part of. And it asks a little of everyone: communities steward themselves, friends vouch for friends, and trust is something you spend and earn rather than a number that goes up.

## The whole thing in one line

**Weft should feel like leaving a note with a trusted friend — brief to write, safe to wait on, and warm when an answer comes back.**
